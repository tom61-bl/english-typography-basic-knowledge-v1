Type Categories

Understanding the Typographic Landscape · 字体的版图

Serif

Old Style

Garamond · Caslon · Jenson

The earliest humanist serifs, inspired by Renaissance calligraphy. Warm, organic strokes with a gentle, pen-drawn quality. Perfect for long-form reading.

Serif

Transitional

Baskerville · Times New Roman · Georgia

The bridge between old and modern. Sharper contrast, more precise geometry, and a refined authority that suits both print and screen.

Serif

Modern / Didone

Bodoni · Didot · Futura*

Extreme contrast between thick and thin strokes. Hairline serifs, dramatic drama. Commands attention in headlines, struggles in body text.

Serif

Slab Serif

Rockwell · Courier · Archer

Bold, rectangular serifs with a mechanical, industrial character. Evokes the typewriter era, poster printing, and vintage Americana.

Sans-Serif

Geometric

Futura · Avant Garde · Circular

Built from pure geometric forms — circles, triangles, straight lines. Clean, modern, and architecturally precise. The Bauhaus spirit in letterform.

Script & Blackletter

Expressive

Lobster · Garamond Italic · Blackletter

The most human of all typefaces. Mimics handwriting, calligraphy, and brushwork. Beautiful in headlines, unreadable in body text.

Garamond

Claude Garamond · 1540

Garamond is perhaps the most beloved typeface in the world. Designed by French punchcutter Claude Garamond in the 1540s, it draws directly from the pen strokes of Renaissance scribes. Its gentle contrast, slightly inclined axis, and open counters make it supremely readable at any size. The British publisher Penguin Books adopted it as their house typeface in 1935 — a decision so right that it defined the brand for decades.

William Caslon's typeface was born in a London foundry in 1722, originally created for a Bible printer. By the 1730s, it had become the standard typeface of English printing — so dominant that the American Declaration of Independence was reportedly printed in Caslon. Its sturdy, slightly irregular character gives text a tactile, almost handmade quality that still feels alive today.

Transitional & Modern Serifs

The Age of Reason Meets the Industrial Revolution

Transitional · English

Baskerville

John Baskerville · 1757

Designer
John Baskerville
Era
Age of Enlightenment · 1757
Mood
Refined · Precise · Authoritative
Used By
Apple · Moleskine · Harvard UP

John Baskerville was a Birmingham calligrapher and typefounder who created what many consider the most refined typeface ever made. Working in the Age of Enlightenment, Baskerville increased the contrast between thick and thin strokes, verticalized the axis, and created razor-sharp serifs — all of which made his type technically superior but controversially "too perfect" for some 18th-century readers. Today, Apple chose Baskerville as the default serif in iOS and macOS, cementing its status as a bridge between classical print and digital design.

Giambattista Bodoni of Parma pushed type design to its most dramatic extreme. Where Garamond whispered, Bodoni shouted — with razor-thin hairline strokes that vanished at small sizes but blazed at display sizes. His Manuale Tipografico of 1818 remains one of the most beautiful books ever printed. Today, the Bodoni family is synonymous with fashion and luxury: Vogue, Harper's Bazaar, Armani, and Dior all rely on Bodoni's dramatic contrast to convey elegance and exclusivity.

Times New Roman

Stanley Morison · 1931

Designer
Stanley Morison for The Times
Era
Modern · 1931
Mood
Functional · Neutral · Ubiquitous
Used By
Microsoft Word · UK Government · Schools

Times New Roman was designed in 1931 specifically for the newspaper The Times of London — not to be beautiful, but to fit as many words as possible in a narrow column while remaining legible at small sizes. Stanley Morison studied the newspaper's existing type and optimized everything for density and efficiency. The result became the default typeface of Microsoft Word in 1992, and for billions of students and office workers, Times New Roman IS what a "normal font" looks like.

Sans-Serif Typefaces

Form Follows Function — The Bauhaus Revolution in Letterform

Grotesque · Swiss

Helvetica

Max Miedinger & Eduard Hoffmann · 1957

Designer
Miedinger & Hoffmann
Origin
Haas Type Foundry, Switzerland
Mood
Neutral · Corporate · Swiss Precision
Used By
NYC Subway · American Airlines · BMW

Helvetica is arguably the most famous typeface in the world. Designed in 1957 in Switzerland, it became the visual language of Swiss International Style — clean, rational, and objective. Its near-perfect neutrality made it the go-to choice for corporations wanting to communicate reliability without personality: American Airlines, NASA, Lufthansa, the NYC subway system, and thousands of government agencies. Whether you love it or find it "the visual equivalent of elevator music," you cannot escape it.

Futura was designed by Paul Renner in 1927, at the height of the Bauhaus movement in Germany. Built entirely from geometric forms — the circle, triangle, and square — it was revolutionary: no curves, no irregularities, pure construction. Unlike Helvetica's neutrality, Futura feels distinctly of the future — progressive, rational, and optimistic. Volkswagen uses it on every car badge; Louis Vuitton uses it in its modern campaigns; Absolut Vodka made it iconic in advertising.

Gill Sans

Designer
Eric Gill

Eric Gill designed Gill Sans in 1928, inspired by the humanist letterforms of the Renaissance rather than geometric abstraction. The result is the warmest of the major sans-serifs — it has the rationality of Futura but with the organic soul of a hand-drawn letter. The London Underground adopted it as its house typeface in 1933, and today it is arguably more recognizable on the streets of London than Helvetica is in New York.

Slab Serif & Expressive

The Industrial Voice — From Typewriters to Tattoo Parlors

Slab Serif · Typewriter

Courier Prime

Adrian Frutiger · IBM Selectric · 1955

Designer
Adrian Frutiger
Origin
IBM Selectric · 1955
Mood
Typewriter · Authentic · Script Feel
Used By
Screenplays · Government forms · Hacker aesthetic

Courier began as the typeface of the IBM Selectric typewriter — the machine that dominated offices from the 1960s to the 1980s. Its monospaced letterforms (each letter occupies equal horizontal space) were a physical necessity of typewriter mechanics, not an aesthetic choice. Today it is the standard format for screenplays (every page equals one minute of screen time by Hollywood convention) and government documents. It whispers of typewriters, late-night screenwriting sessions, and the romance of the written word.

Lobster

Pablo Impallari · 2011 · Google Fonts

Designer
Pablo Impallari
Origin
Argentina · 2011 · Open Source
Mood
Casual · Friendly · Conversational
Used By

Lobster became one of the most viral typefaces of the 2010s when it was released as a free Google Font in 2011. Its connected, brush-like letterforms evoke the casual warmth of a handwritten note — which is exactly why startups, food brands, and social media designers adopted it so enthusiastically. Within months of release, Lobster was appearing on restaurant menus, indie packaging, and thousands of websites worldwide.
