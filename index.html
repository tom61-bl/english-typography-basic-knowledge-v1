Old Style Serifs

Humanism in Metal — Inspired by the Renaissance Quill

Garamond

Claude Garamond · 1540

Garamond is perhaps the most beloved typeface in the world. Designed by French punchcutter Claude Garamond in the 1540s, it draws directly from the pen strokes of Renaissance scribes. Its gentle contrast, slightly inclined axis, and open counters make it supremely readable at any size. Penguin Books adopted it in 1935 — a decision so right that it defined the brand for decades.

William Caslon's typeface was born in a London foundry in 1722, originally created for a Bible printer. By the 1730s it had become the standard typeface of English printing — so dominant that the American Declaration of Independence was reportedly printed in Caslon. Its sturdy, slightly irregular character gives text a tactile, almost handmade quality that still feels alive today.

Transitional & Modern Serifs

The Age of Reason Meets the Industrial Revolution

Transitional · English

Baskerville

John Baskerville · 1757

Designer
John Baskerville
Era
Age of Enlightenment · 1757
Mood
Refined · Precise · Authoritative
Used By
Apple · Moleskine · Harvard UP

John Baskerville was a Birmingham calligrapher who created what many consider the most refined typeface ever made. Baskerville increased the contrast between thick and thin strokes, verticalized the axis, and created razor-sharp serifs. Apple chose Baskerville as the default serif in iOS and macOS, cementing its status as a bridge between classical print and digital design.

Bodoni pushed type design to its most dramatic extreme. Where Garamond whispered, Bodoni shouted — with razor-thin hairline strokes that vanished at small sizes but blazed at display sizes. Today the Bodoni family is synonymous with fashion and luxury: Vogue, Harper's Bazaar, Armani, and Dior all rely on its dramatic contrast to convey elegance and exclusivity.

Times New Roman

Stanley Morison · 1931

Designer
Stanley Morison for The Times

Modern · 1931

Times New Roman was designed specifically for the newspaper The Times of London — not to be beautiful, but to fit as many words as possible in a narrow column while remaining legible at small sizes. It became the default typeface of Microsoft Word in 1992, and for billions of people, Times New Roman IS what a "normal font" looks like.

Sans-Serif Typefaces

Form Follows Function — The Bauhaus Revolution in Letterform

Grotesque · Swiss

Helvetica

Max Miedinger & Eduard Hoffmann · 1957

Designer
Miedinger & Hoffmann
Origin
Haas Type Foundry, Switzerland
Mood
Neutral · Corporate · Swiss
Used By
NYC Subway · American Airlines · BMW

Helvetica is arguably the most famous typeface in the world. Designed in 1957 in Switzerland, it became the visual language of Swiss International Style. Its near-perfect neutrality made it the go-to choice for corporations wanting to communicate reliability without personality. Whether you love it or find it "elevator music," you cannot escape it.

Futura was designed at the height of the Bauhaus movement in Germany. Built entirely from geometric forms — the circle, triangle, and square — it was revolutionary: no curves, no irregularities, pure construction. Unlike Helvetica's neutrality, Futura feels distinctly of the future. Volkswagen uses it on every car badge; Louis Vuitton uses it in modern campaigns.

Eric Gill designed Gill Sans in 1928, inspired by the humanist letterforms of the Renaissance rather than geometric abstraction. The result is the warmest of the major sans-serifs — it has the rationality of Futura but with the organic soul of a hand-drawn letter. The London Underground adopted it as its house typeface in 1933.

Slab Serif & Expressive

The Industrial Voice — From Typewriters to Tattoo Parlors

Adrian Frutiger · IBM Selectric · 1955

Designer
Adrian Frutiger
Origin
IBM Selectric · 1955
Mood

Used By
Screenplays · Government · Hacker aesthetic

Courier began as the typeface of the IBM Selectric typewriter — the machine that dominated offices from the 1960s to the 1980s. Its monospaced letterforms were a physical necessity of typewriter mechanics. Today it is the standard format for screenplays — every page equals one minute of screen time by Hollywood convention.

Lobster became one of the most viral typefaces of the 2010s when it was released as a free Google Font in 2011. Its connected, brush-like letterforms evoke the casual warmth of a handwritten note. Within months of release, Lobster was appearing on restaurant menus, indie packaging, and thousands of websites worldwide.
